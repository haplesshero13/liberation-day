International Women's Liberation Day (IWLD) is celebrated on 12 March, commemorating women's ongoing fight for equality and liberation along with the global women's rights movement. International Women's Liberation Day gives focus to issues such as gender equality, reproductive rights, and ending violence and abuse against women. [1][2]

Overview

International Women's Liberation Day (IWLD) is observed on 12 March each year, building on more than a century of activism that stretches from early twentieth-century labor movements through today's intersectional feminist campaigns. Spurred by the universal female suffrage movement, the tradition of dedicated women's advocacy days originated in labor movements in Europe and North America during the early 20th century. [3][4][5]

While it shares roots with International Women's Day, IWLD sharpens the focus on liberation—the active dismantling of legal, economic, social, and cultural barriers that prevent women from living on equal footing with men. The date of 12 March was chosen to extend the spirit of solidarity that already surrounds the first half of March.

History & Origins

Early Labor Roots

The earliest forerunner was a "Woman's Day" organized by the Socialist Party of America in New York City on 28 February 1909, drawing together working-class women to demand better wages, shorter hours, and the right to vote. [6]

In solidarity with that effort, communist activist and politician Clara Zetkin proposed a celebration of "Working Women's Day" at the 1910 International Socialist Women's Conference in Copenhagen. The proposal was approved—though no fixed date was set—and the following year saw the first demonstrations and commemorations across Europe. [7]

Recognition and Growth

Vladimir Lenin declared 8 March as International Women's Day in 1922 to honor the role of women in the 1917 Russian Revolution. [8] The holiday was subsequently celebrated by socialist movements and communist countries, and was promoted by the United Nations in 1977. [9] International Women's Liberation Day honours that same lineage while renewing its call: freedom is incomplete until it belongs to everyone.

Focus Areas

IWLD organizers and participants concentrate on issues including, but not limited to:

  • Gender equality in pay, opportunity, and representation
  • Reproductive rights and access to healthcare
  • Ending gender-based violence and abuse
  • Economic empowerment and closing the wealth gap
  • Legal reform to remove discriminatory laws worldwide
  • Education and visibility for women's contributions to history

Observance

Like its predecessor, International Women's Liberation Day is observed through public demonstrations, community events, workplace conversations, social-media campaigns, and governmental or UN-affiliated programs. The day is anchored to a fresh annual theme that highlights a particular dimension of women's liberation.

International Women's Liberation Day is intended to be a public holiday, a day of reflection, and a starting pistol—a reminder that progress made is progress to protect, and progress needed is progress to demand.
